# Samjoko (삼족오)

## Overview
The Samjoko, also known as the Three-Legged Crow or Samjok-o, is a legendary three-legged crow from Korean mythology that resides within the sun. This sacred bird appears prominently in East Asian mythology, particularly in Korean, Chinese, and Japanese traditions, where it represents the sun, divine power, and royal authority. In Korean culture specifically, the Samjoko symbolizes the sun god's messenger, the power of the heavens, and the legitimacy of royal rule. Archaeological evidence of Samjoko imagery in ancient Korean kingdoms demonstrates its deep historical significance in Korean cosmology and political symbolism.

## Description

### Physical Characteristics
- **Legs**: Three legs arranged in tripod formation
- **Body**: Crow or raven-like bird, often larger than natural crows
- **Color**: Black or dark with fiery red or gold highlights
- **Eyes**: Bright, often depicted as glowing or flame-like
- **Aura**: Surrounded by solar energy, flames, or radiant light
- **Size**: Varies from normal crow size to enormous celestial proportions
- **Wings**: Powerful wings capable of traversing the heavens

### Symbolic Elements
- **Three Legs**: Represent heaven, earth, and humanity or past, present, and future
- **Solar Connection**: Lives within the sun, controlling its movements
- **Divine Nature**: Messenger between celestial and earthly realms
- **Royal Association**: Symbol of legitimate rulership and divine mandate
- **Fire Affinity**: Associated with light, heat, and purification

## Historical and Archaeological Evidence

### Goguryeo Kingdom (37 BCE - 668 CE)
The Samjoko appears extensively in Goguryeo art:
- **Tomb Murals**: Numerous depictions in royal tombs
- **Anak Tomb No. 3**: Features Samjoko opposite a moon toad
- **Muyongchong (Tomb of the Dancers)**: Shows Samjoko in solar context
- **Jinpa-ri Tomb**: Displays intricate Samjoko imagery
- **Royal Symbolism**: Associated with Goguryeo royal authority

### Silla Kingdom (57 BCE - 935 CE)
Evidence from ancient Silla:
- **Gold Crowns**: Samjoko motifs on royal headgear
- **Cheonmachong (Heavenly Horse Tomb)**: Three-legged crow imagery
- **Buddhist Temples**: Integration into religious art
- **Royal Seals**: Used in official governmental symbols
- **Hwangnyongsa Temple**: Architectural decorations

### Baekje Kingdom (18 BCE - 660 CE)
Baekje artistic traditions:
- **Tomb Murals**: Similar to Goguryeo representations
- **Royal Artifacts**: Crowns and ceremonial objects
- **Export Art**: Samjoko imagery sent to Japan
- **Buddhist Integration**: Blend of native and Buddhist symbolism

## East Asian Comparative Traditions

### Chinese Yangwu (陽烏)
The Chinese three-legged crow:
- **Origins**: Ancient Chinese solar mythology
- **Mythology**: Ten suns, each with a crow, causing disasters
- **Archer Yi**: Shot down nine suns, leaving one
- **Influence**: Direct influence on Korean traditions
- **Differences**: Korean version more focused on single solar crow

### Japanese Yatagarasu (八咫烏)
The Japanese three-legged crow:
- **Role**: Divine guide and messenger
- **Shintoism**: Sacred to Amaterasu, sun goddess
- **Historical**: Guided Emperor Jimmu in founding Japan
- **Modern**: Symbol of Japan Football Association
- **Korean Connection**: Likely derived from Korean Baekje influence

### Comparative Analysis
Similarities and differences:
- **Shared Origins**: Common East Asian mythological roots
- **Cultural Adaptation**: Each culture developed unique interpretations
- **Solar Association**: All connected to sun mythology
- **Divine Nature**: All serve as heavenly messengers
- **Modern Usage**: Different contemporary applications

## Legends and Folklore

### The First Sunrise
A creation myth tells:
- The world was once in darkness
- The Samjoko brought the first sun
- Its three legs created stable solar movement
- Grateful humans worshipped the bird
- Royal lines claimed descent from solar deity

### The Ten Suns
Korean adaptation of Chinese tale:
- Multiple suns threatened to scorch the earth
- Each contained a Samjoko
- A divine archer saved the world
- One sun and its crow remained
- Established proper cosmic order

### The King and the Crow
A Goguryeo legend:
- A righteous king received visions from a Samjoko
- The bird guided him to victory in war
- Enemies fled when they saw the solar bird
- The kingdom prospered under heavenly favor
- The king's descendants used the crow as their symbol
